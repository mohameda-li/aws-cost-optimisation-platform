Cloud storage and why it matters

Cloud storage is the way organisations keep data in the cloud rather than on local servers. It includes files, logs, backups, databases, container images and system metrics. The benefit is flexibility and scale, but the trade off is that storage can grow quietly, and the cost can rise without anyone noticing until it becomes significant.

How fast is cloud data growing

Global data volumes are rising quickly, driven by analytics, security logging, AI workloads and always on services. Storage is often the easiest resource to ignore because nothing breaks when it increases, but the bill still grows.

Zettabyte-scale growth

This trend shows how quickly global data creation is accelerating. In practice, a large portion of that growth becomes operational storage: logs, backups, analytics outputs and application data. The result is that storage spend can increase quietly over time unless retention, tiering and rightsizing are reviewed continuously.

What the platform covers

The platform scans five widely used AWS services and produces an email report with recommendations, estimated monthly savings, and practical next steps. Each service is analysed using a consistent pipeline, so results are easy to compare.

Amazon S3

Object storage used for logs, backups, files, data lakes and application assets. The scan focuses on inactive data, storage class opportunities and buckets that may be costing money without delivering ongoing value.

Example recommendations: storage tiering, lifecycle policies and retention clean up.

Amazon RDS

Managed relational databases used for production systems, internal tools and analytics. The scan targets signs of overprovisioning and recommends rightsizing where performance risk is low.

Example recommendations: smaller instance classes, stopping unused environments and reviewing storage allocation.

Amazon ECS

Container orchestration for microservices and batch tasks. The scan highlights services with low utilisation and oversized CPU and memory settings, which can lead to wasted capacity.

Example recommendations: task rightsizing, scaling policy checks and idle service detection.

Amazon EKS

Managed Kubernetes for containerised workloads. The scan focuses on cluster efficiency signals, node capacity drift and opportunities to reduce overprovisioned compute.

Example recommendations: node rightsizing signals, scaling adjustments and capacity reduction opportunities.

EC2 Spot Instances

Discounted compute capacity for interruption tolerant workloads. The scan identifies workloads that may be suitable for Spot and estimates potential savings compared with on demand pricing.

Example recommendations: Spot adoption for eligible workloads, diversification strategies and interruption planning.
